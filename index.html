1. Your Learning Goal

This guide teaches you, step by step, how to write a strong IELTS Academic Writing Task 1 answer about pie charts. You are starting at around Band 5, and the goal is to build the habits that examiners reward at Band 6.5–7.

Good news: pie chart answers follow a very repeatable pattern. You do not need difficult grammar or rare vocabulary. You need a clear structure, a good overview, sensible grouping of data, and accurate simple sentences. This guide gives you all four.

How to use this guide: work through it slowly — one or two sections per study session, not everything at once. Come back and re-do the practice tasks a few days later. Reviewing after a gap is how the language actually sticks.

2. What Is a Pie Chart Question?

A pie chart is a circle divided into slices. The whole circle = 100%. Each slice shows one category, and the size of the slice shows its proportion (its share of the total).

Pie chart questions usually ask you to describe:

What pie charts showExample
Percentages — each slice has a number like 40%"Cars: 42%"
Proportions — how big each part is compared to the whole"Cars made up the largest share of journeys."
Categories — the different groups in the chartTransport types, energy sources, spending areas
Comparisons — which categories are bigger or smaller"The figure for buses was twice as high as for trains."
Changes — only when there are two or more charts for different times"Coal fell from 40% to 15% between 2000 and 2020."
One chart or several? This changes your language completely.
• One pie chart (or several charts for the same time): compare categories only. Do NOT use "increased" or "decreased" — nothing changes over time.
• Two or three charts for different years: you can (and should) describe changes: "rose", "fell", "doubled".

3. What the Examiner Wants

Your Task 1 answer is marked on four criteria, each worth 25%. Here is what each one means in simple language, what Band 5 answers usually do wrong, and how to move up.

3.1 Task Achievement (TA) — "Did you do the task properly?"

Details
What it measuresDid you describe the chart accurately, include an overview, choose the key information, and write at least 150 words?
Band 5 problemNo overview paragraph; lists every number mechanically; adds opinions or reasons that are not in the chart; sometimes under 150 words.
Band 6–7 behaviourClear overview of the main features; selects the biggest, smallest and most interesting comparisons; all data accurate; no opinions.
Improvement tipsAlways write a separate overview paragraph starting with "Overall, ...". Choose 6–9 numbers to mention, not all of them. Never explain why something happens — only what the chart shows.

3.2 Coherence and Cohesion (CC) — "Is it organised and easy to follow?"

Details
What it measuresParagraphing, logical order, and linking words (however, while, in contrast).
Band 5 problemOne long paragraph, or sentences in random order; repeats "and... and... also... also"; linking words used wrongly or too often.
Band 6–7 behaviourFour clear paragraphs; data grouped logically (e.g. big categories together); a variety of linkers used correctly and naturally.
Improvement tipsUse the 4-paragraph plan in Section 4 every single time. Learn 6–8 linkers well (while, whereas, in contrast, similarly, however) instead of 20 badly.
Turkish L1 alert: avoid translating Turkish connectors directly. "In addition to this situation..." sounds unnatural in English. Use a short, simple linker instead: "Furthermore, ..." or "In addition, ...".

3.3 Lexical Resource (LR) — "Is your vocabulary varied and accurate?"

Details
What it measuresThe range and accuracy of your words, and whether you can paraphrase instead of copying the question.
Band 5 problemRepeats the same words ("show", "percent", "big") again and again; copies whole phrases from the question; wrong word forms ("the percentage of car is bigger").
Band 6–7 behaviourUses a range of chart language: "accounted for", "made up", "the largest proportion", "just under half"; paraphrases the question in the introduction.
Improvement tipsLearn the vocabulary tables in Section 9 in small groups (5–7 phrases at a time). Practise swapping "percent" for fractions: 50% = "half", 25% = "a quarter".

3.4 Grammatical Range and Accuracy (GRA) — "Are your sentences correct and varied?"

Details
What it measuresSentence variety (simple + complex) and how many errors you make.
Band 5 problemOnly short simple sentences, or long sentences full of errors; missing articles ("the"); wrong tense; subject–verb mistakes ("The figures shows").
Band 6–7 behaviourA mix of simple and complex sentences (using "while", "whereas", "which"); most sentences error-free; correct past tense for past years.
Improvement tipsMaster 5–6 sentence patterns (Section 10) until they are automatic. Accuracy first, variety second. Check articles and verb endings in your final 3 minutes.
Turkish L1 alert: Turkish has no articles, so "the" often disappears: "Largest proportion was for cars" ✗ → "The largest proportion was for cars" ✓. Also, if the chart shows a past year (e.g. 2020), use the past tense: "accounted for", not "accounts for".

4. The Ideal 4-Paragraph Structure

Use this same structure for every pie chart answer. Examiners recognise it instantly, and it makes planning fast.

ParagraphNameWhat it containsLength
1IntroductionOne or two sentences that paraphrase the question: what the chart shows, where, and when. No data.1–2 sentences (20–30 words)
2OverviewThe big picture: the largest category, the smallest, and the main comparison or change. No exact numbers. Start with "Overall, ...".1–2 sentences (25–40 words)
3Main details 1The first logical group of data — usually the largest categories — with exact percentages and comparisons.3–4 sentences (50–60 words)
4Main details 2The second group — usually the smaller categories, or the second time period — with exact percentages.3–4 sentences (50–60 words)
Why this works: the introduction and overview cover Task Achievement; the two grouped body paragraphs cover Coherence and Cohesion. That is half your marks organised before you write a single detail.

5. The 10-Step Method for Any Pie Chart Question

Follow these steps in order every time. With practice, steps 1–7 take about five minutes.

StepWhat to doWhy it matters
1. Read the questionRead the full instruction sentence carefully, twice.It tells you exactly what the charts show. Misreading it damages Task Achievement.
2. Identify the topicWhat is the chart about? (transport, energy, spending...)You will paraphrase this in your introduction.
3. Identify the time periodIs there a year? Two years? No time at all?Past year → past tense. Two years → change language. No year → present tense, no change language.
4. Identify the unitsAlmost always percentages (%) in pie charts — but check.Writing "42 people" when the chart shows "42%" is a data error.
5. Find the biggest categoriesCircle the one or two largest slices in each chart.These go in your overview and body paragraph 1.
6. Find the smallest categoriesCircle the smallest slice in each chart.The contrast between biggest and smallest is your easiest comparison.
7. Notice similarities, differences or changesWhich categories are similar in size? Which changed most between charts?This gives you the "main trend" for the overview.
8. Write the overview BEFORE the detailsWrite "Overall, ..." immediately after the introduction.If you run out of time, the overview is already there. No overview = Band 5 for TA.
9. Group information logicallyDecide your two groups (e.g. large vs small; year 1 vs year 2) before writing the bodies.Logical grouping is the heart of Coherence and Cohesion.
10. Check grammar, word count and clarityIn the last 3 minutes: articles, verb tense, subject–verb agreement, 150+ words.Fixing five small errors can lift GRA by half a band.

6. How to Write the Introduction

Paraphrasing means saying the same thing with different words. You must not copy the question sentence — copied words do not count towards your 150 words, and copying hurts Lexical Resource.

You only need to change two or three things: the verb ("shows" → "compares"), the noun ("pie charts" → "charts"), and the description of the content.

Useful introduction phrases

Question says...You can write...
The pie chart shows...The chart illustrates... / The chart gives information about...
The pie charts show...The pie charts compare... / The two charts illustrate...
...the proportion of......the percentage of... / ...how much... / ...the share of...
...in 2010 and 2020...over a ten-year period / ...between 2010 and 2020 / ...in two different years
The charts illustrate...The diagrams present information about... / The charts display...
...how people travel to work...the different ways in which people commute / ...the transport methods people use to get to work
Simple formula: The chart(s) illustrate(s) / compare(s) + what + where + when.
Example "The pie chart illustrates how adults in one UK city travelled to work in 2024."
Avoid Copying: "The pie chart shows how adults in one UK city travelled to work in 2024." — identical to the question except one word. The examiner will ignore it.

7. How to Write the Overview

The overview is the most important paragraph in Task 1. The official band descriptors say a Band 7 answer "presents a clear overview of main trends, differences or stages". Without an overview, Task Achievement is capped at Band 5 — no matter how good your English is.

What to mention (choose 2–3 of these)

FeatureExample phrase
The biggest category"cars were by far the most common form of transport"
The smallest category"only a small minority chose to cycle"
The main trend"renewable energy grew dramatically"
A major comparison"private transport was far more popular than public transport"
The overall change (two time periods)"the country moved away from fossil fuels towards renewables"

What NOT to do in the overview

  • ❌ Do not include exact numbers — save them for the body paragraphs.
  • ❌ Do not give opinions ("I think this is because...").
  • ❌ Do not explain reasons unless the chart itself shows them.
  • ❌ Do not miss the overview — this is the single most expensive mistake at Band 5.
Weak overview "Overall, cars are 42%, buses are 20%, trains are 14%, bicycles are 10%, walking is 9% and other is 5%."
Problem: this is a list of numbers, not a big picture. It repeats the body paragraphs and shows no ability to summarise.
Strong overview "Overall, the car was by far the most popular way of getting to work, while cycling and walking were chosen by only small minorities. Public transport together accounted for roughly a third of all journeys."
Why it works: biggest feature + smallest features + one smart grouping, and no exact numbers.

8. How to Write the Body Paragraphs

Never describe slices in random order. Choose a grouping method first, then give each group one paragraph. Here are five reliable methods:

Grouping methodWhen to use itExample plan
Biggest vs smallestOne chart with clear large and small slicesPara 3: car (42%) + bus (20%). Para 4: bicycle, walking, other (all under 15%).
Similar categories togetherCategories that belong to a natural familyPara 3: private transport (car, bicycle). Para 4: public transport (bus, train).
Increases vs decreasesTwo charts over timePara 3: what rose (renewables). Para 4: what fell (coal, nuclear).
Time period 1 vs time period 2Two charts, when many things changedPara 3: the situation in 2000. Para 4: the situation in 2020.
More popular vs less popularCharts about choices or preferencesPara 3: the two favourite activities. Para 4: the minority activities.
Example body paragraph (biggest group) "The car dominated the chart, accounting for 42% of all journeys to work. Buses were the second most common option, at 20%, which was almost half the figure for cars. Trains made up a further 14%."
Notice: exact numbers, a comparison ("almost half"), and linking that flows naturally.
Turkish L1 alert: keep to English subject–verb–object order in comparisons. "Buses 20% with second most common option was" follows Turkish word order. English: "Buses were the second most common option, at 20%."

10. Grammar for Pie Charts

You do not need many structures — you need a few structures used accurately. Master these six patterns:

PatternExample
X accounted for [%] of YCars accounted for 42% of journeys to work.
The largest proportion was for X, at [%]The largest proportion was for housing, at 35%.
X was twice as high as YThe figure for cars was twice as high as the figure for buses.
The figure for X increased from [%] to [%]The figure for renewables increased from 10% to 45%.
While X rose, Y fellWhile the share of recycling rose, the share of landfill fell.
X was the most common, whereas Y was the least commonDriving was the most common method, whereas walking was the least common.

Common Band 5 grammar mistakes — and the fix

Incorrect sentenceCorrect sentenceExplanation
Car is 42%.Cars accounted for 42% of journeys.A car is not a percentage. Use "accounted for / made up" and a plural or category noun.
The figures shows that coal is decrease.The figures show that coal decreased.Subject–verb agreement ("figures show") and verb form ("decreased", not "is decrease").
Largest proportion was for housing.The largest proportion was for housing.Superlatives always need "the". (Turkish has no articles — check every superlative.)
The chart shows information about how people travelled in 2024. Cars accounts for 42%....Cars accounted for 42%.2024 is finished, so use past tense consistently through the whole answer.
Buses were 20% more than trains.The figure for buses was 6 percentage points higher than that for trains."20% more" means a different thing mathematically. Compare figures carefully, or write "higher than".
The percentage of people who uses bus is 20%.The percentage of people who used buses was 20%.Relative clause verb must agree ("people who used") and match the tense.
Renewables was increased to 45%.Renewables increased to 45%."Increase" here is not passive. The thing increases by itself: "X increased".
%42 of people went by car.42% of people went by car.In English the % symbol comes after the number — the opposite of Turkish (%42 → 42%).

11. How to Describe Percentages Naturally

If you write "percent" in every sentence, your Lexical Resource score suffers. Swap some percentages for fractions and natural expressions:

PercentageNatural expressionExample sentence
50%halfHalf of all journeys were made by car.
25%a quarterA quarter of the budget went on food.
33%one third / roughly one thirdRoughly one third of electricity came from gas.
75%three quartersThree quarters of waste was sent to landfill.
10%one in tenOne in ten commuters cycled to work.
66%two thirdsTwo thirds of visitors were under 50.
20%one fifth / a fifthA fifth of journeys were made by bus.
48% / 52%just under half / just over halfJust under half of the population drove to work.
9%almost a tenth / fewer than one in tenFewer than one in ten people walked.
Rule of thumb: use the exact percentage the first time, then a natural expression for nearby or repeated figures. Mix both across your answer.

12. Sample Question 1 — One Pie Chart

Question: The pie chart below shows how adults in one UK city travelled to work in 2024. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant. Write at least 150 words.
Method of travelPercentage
Car42%
Bus20%
Train14%
Bicycle10%
Walking9%
Other5%

Step-by-step analysis

StepResult
TopicHow adults travelled to work in one UK city
Time period2024 — one year in the past → past tense, no change language
UnitsPercentages
BiggestCar (42%) — more than double the next category
SmallestOther (5%), then walking (9%)
Key comparisonsCar ≈ bus + train together (34%); bicycle and walking similar in size (10% vs 9%)
Grouping planPara 3: car, bus, train (motorised, big). Para 4: bicycle, walking, other (small).

Band 5 sample answer

The pie chart shows how adults in one UK city travelled to work in 2024. Car is the biggest with 42 percent. Bus is 20 percent and train is 14 percent. Bicycle is 10 percent. Walking is 9 percent and other is 5 percent.

I think many people use car because it is more comfortable and the weather in UK is very rainy. Bus is also popular because it is cheap. Train is expensive so less people use it. Bicycle and walking is healthy but people are lazy.

In conclusion, car is the most popular and other is the least popular transport in this city.

≈105 words — already under the 150 minimum

Problems with the Band 5 answer

  • TA: under 150 words; no real overview; gives opinions and invented reasons ("weather is rainy", "people are lazy") that are not in the chart.
  • TA: the introduction copies the question word for word.
  • CC: data listed mechanically in chart order — no grouping, no comparisons.
  • LR: "is + percent" repeated six times; "biggest", "popular" repeated.
  • GRA: "Car is the biggest" (article/number problems); "Bicycle and walking is" (agreement); "less people" → "fewer people"; present tense for a past year.

Improved Band 7 sample answer

The pie chart illustrates the different methods of transport that adults in one British city used to commute in 2024.

Overall, the car was by far the most common way of getting to work, while cycling and walking were chosen by only small minorities. Public transport, taken together, accounted for roughly a third of all journeys.

Driving dominated the chart, accounting for 42% of journeys to work. Buses were the second most popular option, at 20%, which was slightly less than half the figure for cars. Trains carried a further 14% of commuters, meaning that buses and trains combined were almost as significant as the car alone.

The remaining methods each represented a tenth of the total or less. One in ten adults cycled to work, and a similar proportion, 9%, walked. Other methods of travel made up the smallest share, at just 5%, less than an eighth of the figure for driving.

≈153 words

Sentence-by-sentence explanation

SentenceWhat it does
"The pie chart illustrates... used to commute in 2024."Paraphrases the question: "shows"→"illustrates", "travelled to work"→"used to commute", "UK"→"British".
"Overall, the car was by far the most common..."Overview: biggest feature + smallest features + a smart grouping ("public transport... a third"). No exact numbers.
"Driving dominated the chart, accounting for 42%..."Starts body 1 with the biggest category; "accounting for" adds a participle clause (complex grammar).
"Buses were the second most popular option, at 20%, which was..."Ranking language + "at 20%" + a relative clause comparison ("slightly less than half").
"Trains carried a further 14%, meaning that...""a further" avoids repeating "also"; the clause draws a comparison across categories.
"One in ten adults cycled... a similar proportion, 9%, walked."Natural percentage language ("one in ten") and variety ("a similar proportion").
"Other methods... at just 5%, less than an eighth of the figure for driving."Ends with the smallest share and one final precise comparison.

13. Sample Question 2 — Two Pie Charts (Two Years)

Question: The pie charts below show the main sources of electricity in one European country in 2000 and 2020. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant. Write at least 150 words.
Source20002020
Coal40%15%
Gas25%20%
Nuclear20%15%
Renewables10%45%
Oil5%5%

Step-by-step analysis

StepResult
TopicSources of electricity in one European country
Time period2000 and 2020 → past tense + change language allowed
UnitsPercentages of electricity production
Biggest2000: coal (40%). 2020: renewables (45%) — the leader changed!
SmallestOil in both years (5%) — unchanged
Main changesRenewables rose ×4.5 (10→45); coal fell by more than half (40→15); gas and nuclear fell slightly; oil stable
Grouping planPara 3: what increased (renewables) + the change of leader. Para 4: what fell or stayed the same (coal, gas, nuclear, oil).

Band 5 sample answer

The pie charts show the main sources of electricity in one European country in 2000 and 2020. In 2000 coal is 40%, gas is 25%, nuclear is 20%, renewables is 10% and oil is 5%. In 2020 coal is 15%, gas is 20%, nuclear is 15%, renewables is 45% and oil is 5%.

Coal decreased and renewables increased. Gas decreased a little bit and also nuclear decreased a little bit. Oil is same. I think this country wants to protect environment so they use more renewable energy. This is good decision because coal makes pollution.

≈95 words — well under the 150 minimum

Problems with the Band 5 answer

  • TA: well under 150 words; no overview paragraph; opinions and reasons added ("wants to protect environment", "good decision").
  • TA: introduction copied directly from the question.
  • CC: every number listed twice in chart order — no selection, no grouping; "and also... and also" repetition.
  • LR: "is + %" ten times; "decreased a little bit" is informal and repeated.
  • GRA: present tense for past years; "Oil is same" → "Oil remained the same"; missing articles ("the environment", "a good decision").

Improved Band 7 sample answer

The pie charts compare five sources of electricity in a European country in two years, 2000 and 2020.

Overall, the country moved away from fossil fuels over the period. Renewable energy grew dramatically to become the leading source of electricity, largely replacing coal, which lost more than half of its share.

The most striking change was in renewables, whose share more than quadrupled, from just 10% in 2000 to 45% in 2020. As a result, renewable sources overtook coal, which had been the dominant source at the start of the period, accounting for 40% of production.

All the remaining sources either declined or remained stable. Coal fell sharply to only 15% by 2020, while the figures for gas and nuclear power dropped more modestly, from 25% to 20% and from 20% to 15% respectively. Oil was the only source whose share was unchanged, contributing a mere 5% of electricity in both years.

≈152 words

Sentence-by-sentence explanation

SentenceWhat it does
"The pie charts compare five sources... in two years..."Paraphrase: "show"→"compare", "main sources"→"five sources", "in 2000 and 2020"→"in two years, 2000 and 2020".
"Overall, the country moved away from fossil fuels..."Overview gives the big story (the overall change) without exact numbers.
"...whose share more than quadrupled, from just 10%... to 45%..."Body 1 leads with the biggest change; "whose" = complex relative clause; "from X to Y" pattern.
"...renewable sources overtook coal, which had been the dominant source...""overtook" is precise change vocabulary; past perfect ("had been") shows an earlier time correctly.
"All the remaining sources either declined or remained stable."Topic sentence that groups paragraph 4 logically ("either... or").
"...from 25% to 20% and from 20% to 15% respectively.""respectively" reports two figures efficiently — a Band 7 economy device.
"Oil was the only source whose share was unchanged..."Ends with the exception; "a mere 5%" is small-proportion language.

14. Sample Question 3 — Three Pie Charts

Question: The pie charts below show how average household income was spent in three countries in 2023. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant. Write at least 150 words.
Spending categoryCountry A (UK)Country B (Turkey)Country C (Japan)
Housing35%25%30%
Food20%32%24%
Transport15%14%20%
Leisure18%12%13%
Other12%17%13%

Step-by-step analysis

StepResult
TopicHousehold spending in three countries
Time period2023 — one past year → past tense, no change language (three places, not three times!)
UnitsPercentages of household income
BiggestHousing in the UK (35%) and Japan (30%); food in Turkey (32%)
SmallestLeisure in Turkey (12%); "other" in the UK (12%)
Key comparisonsHousing and food together = over half everywhere; Turkey spends most on food, least on leisure; transport highest in Japan
Grouping planPara 3: the two dominant categories (housing + food) across all three countries. Para 4: the smaller categories (transport, leisure, other).

Band 7 sample answer

The pie charts illustrate the proportion of household income that families in the UK, Turkey and Japan spent on five categories in 2023.

Overall, housing and food were the two largest areas of expenditure in every country, together taking over half of the average budget. The most noticeable difference was that Turkish households spent considerably more on food, whereas British households devoted the largest share to housing.

Housing was the biggest single expense in the UK and Japan, at 35% and 30% respectively, but only the second biggest in Turkey, at a quarter of income. The pattern for food was almost the reverse: it accounted for nearly one third of spending in Turkey, compared with 24% in Japan and just a fifth in the UK.

The remaining categories were smaller and broadly similar across the three countries. Transport represented between 14% and 20% of budgets, with Japanese families spending the most. Leisure was highest in the UK, at 18%, while in Turkey it made up the smallest share of all, at 12%.

≈172 words

Explanation of structure and language

FeatureHow the answer does it
StructureClassic 4 paragraphs. With three charts, the grouping is by category (big spending areas vs small ones), not chart-by-chart — describing each pie separately would create three repetitive paragraphs.
OverviewFinds what is common to all three charts (housing + food dominate) and the most interesting difference (Turkey's food spending). No exact numbers.
No change languageThe charts show three countries at the same time, so the answer never says "increased" — it says "higher", "more", "the reverse". This is a classic trap.
Comparative grammar"considerably more", "almost the reverse", "compared with", "respectively", "between 14% and 20%" — comparisons across all three data sets.
Percentage variety"a quarter", "nearly one third", "just a fifth" replace repeated "%".
CoverageEvery category is mentioned at least once, but not every number — 12 of the 15 figures appear directly or through approximation. Selection, not listing.

Study how each weak sentence is rebuilt. The pattern is always the same: precise verb + exact figure + comparison or natural expression.

17. Writing Templates

These templates give you a skeleton, not a script. Change the [bracketed] parts and adapt the sentence patterns — examiners penalise obviously memorised language, so practise making each template your own.

Template A — One pie chart (single past year)

P1: The pie chart illustrates [what] in [place] in [year].

P2: Overall, [biggest category] was by far the most [common/significant], while [smallest categories] accounted for only small proportions.

P3: [Biggest category] dominated, accounting for [X]% of the total. [Second category] was the next most [common], at [Y]%, which was [comparison with biggest].

P4: The remaining categories each represented [fraction] or less. [Category] made up [Z]%, while [smallest] held the smallest share, at just [W]%.

Template B — Two pie charts (two years)

P1: The pie charts compare [what] in [place] in [year 1] and [year 2].

P2: Overall, [main change — what grew / what declined], while [what stayed stable]. [Optional: the leading category changed from X to Y.]

P3: The most striking change was in [category], whose share [rose/fell] from [X]% to [Y]%. As a result, [consequence for ranking — e.g. it overtook Z].

P4: In contrast, [declining/stable categories]. [Category] dropped from [X]% to [Y]%, while the figure for [category] remained [stable/unchanged], at [Z]%.

Template C — Three pie charts (three places or groups, same time)

P1: The pie charts illustrate [what] in [place A], [place B] and [place C] in [year].

P2: Overall, [what is common to all three charts], although [the most interesting difference between them].

P3: [The dominant category/categories]: it was highest in [place], at [X]%, compared with [Y]% in [place B] and [Z]% in [place C].

P4: The smaller categories were [broadly similar / more varied]. [Category] ranged from [X]% to [Y]%, with [place] recording the [highest/lowest] figure.

Template D — Pie charts showing change over time (general)

P1: The charts show how [what] changed between [year 1] and [year 2] in [place].

P2: Overall, there was a clear shift from [old pattern] towards [new pattern] over the period.

P3 (increases): The categories that grew were [list]. [Biggest riser] climbed from [X]% to [Y]%, [multiplying / more than doubling] its share.

P4 (decreases/stable): Meanwhile, [category] lost ground, falling from [X]% to [Y]%, and [category] was virtually unchanged at around [Z]%.

Template E — Pie chart with no time period given

Use the present simple throughout, and no change language.

P1: The pie chart illustrates [what] in [place].

P2: Overall, [biggest category] accounts for the largest proportion, whereas [smallest] makes up only a tiny fraction.

P3: [Biggest category] represents [X]% of the total, [comparison] the figure for [second category], at [Y]%.

P4: At the other end of the scale, [small categories] together constitute [approximation], with [smallest] contributing just [Z]%.

21. Final Summary — The Golden Rules

  1. Four paragraphs, always: introduction → overview → details 1 → details 2.
  2. The overview is king. Write it early, start it with "Overall", give the big picture with no numbers.
  3. Select, don't list. Report the biggest, the smallest and the best comparisons — not every figure.
  4. Compare constantly: twice as high as, slightly higher than, whereas, compared with.
  5. Watch the time period: past year → past tense; two+ periods → change language; one chart → no "increased".
  6. No opinions, no reasons, no predictions. Report only what the chart shows.
  7. Vary your percentage language: half, a quarter, one in ten, just under half.
  8. Accuracy beats ambition: six correct sentence patterns are worth more than sixteen broken ones.
  9. Check articles and tenses in your final 3 minutes — the highest-value fix for Turkish speakers.
  10. 160–180 words, 20 minutes, then stop. Task 2 is waiting.

Work through this guide patiently. A move from Band 5 to Band 6.5–7 in Writing is a real, achievable goal — but it is built over months of steady practice, not days. Each practice task you complete with the checklist takes you one step closer.
